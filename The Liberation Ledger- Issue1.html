Ledger of Lies

Capitalism keeps its books in ledgers etched from human labor, measuring worth in extraction and exhaustion. Its arithmetic is simple: the few who own, the many who toil.

We reject these books of bondage.

Beyond the Shell Game

The state and market mirror each other—two custodians guarding the same hierarchy. Rearranging power within the same frame does nothing to alter the frame itself.

We refuse their rigged board.

Ledger of Liberation

Our new ledger is written in community: neighbors feeding one another, care offered without transaction, power built horizontally. This is the currency that cannot be hoarded.

Against the Machinery

Landlords hoard shelter. Bosses siphon labor. Corporations strip land for profit. Yet their empire is already fissuring—fractured by its own insatiability.

Mutual Aid as Rebellion

Rebellion begins with meeting needs: shared food, shared care, shared tools, shared time. Mutual aid is not charity—it is infrastructure for a world without extraction.

The Anti-Capitalist Economy

We build networks of co-ops, neighborhood assemblies, repair cycles, and collective stewardship. This is not a utopia—it is a blueprint.

Ritual of Refusal

We reclaim color, story, and rhythm from the gray logic of capital. We pledge allegiance to each other, to land, to memory—not to profit.

The Hand That Feeds

How Capitalism Devalues Care Work While Depending on It for Survival

Beneath the gleam of skyscrapers and the fevered hum of stock markets pulses a quieter, more essential labor: care work. The paradox is glaring but rarely acknowledged — capitalism, for all its idolization of productivity and profit, is entirely dependent on the daily, sometimes invisible, labor of caregivers: nurses, teachers, childcare workers, home health aides, cleaning staff, and countless others whose work sustains social and biological life. Yet, in the late capitalist era, we witness a fresh assault: the declassification and delegitimization of these professions, even as the market heaps legitimacy and reward upon profit-driven occupations. Contemporary capitalism not only undervalues care work but systemically undermines its legitimacy, despite its existential indispensability.

The Architecture of Disregard

Capitalism’s foundational premise is straightforward: value is determined by what can be bought and sold. But what happens when the most vital work — feeding, teaching, healing, nurturing — cannot be efficiently monetized? The answer is a social and economic order that simultaneously relies on and relegates care. Wages for care workers trail far behind those in finance, technology, or management, even as demand for their labor rises in aging societies, in schools, hospitals, and homes.

During COVID-19, it became vogue to call healthcare workers “heroes,” but society quickly revealed the limits of its gratitude. “Hero” is a cheap wage supplement. Once the clapping faded, the underpayment, overwork, and disregard returned. As soon as the market’s machinery could grind back to “normal,” those who kept it running were thanked, patted on the back, and ushered back to the shadows. What capitalism cannot commodify, it is eager to ignore, or worse, exploit.

Declassification: Stripping Legitimacy, Eroding Security

Now, a subtler and more insidious shift is underway: the “declassification” of care work. Roles once understood as skilled and indispensable are bureaucratically recast as “unskilled” or “entry-level.” With the stroke of a pen, classroom aides, elder care workers, and even some nursing staff are recategorized — benefits evaporate, protections wane. This isn’t just a matter of wages; it is a calculated erosion of dignity and legitimacy.

Meanwhile, the market lionizes the profit-chasers: investment bankers, consultants, tech entrepreneurs. Occupations that extract value — often by leveraging or outright exploiting the labor of caregivers — are dubbed “real” work. The supposed meritocracy of the market turns out to be a hierarchy of profit extraction, not social necessity.

Who built the “meritocracy,” anyway? The financiers and founders, those titans of capitalism, are products of years — decades — of care. The invisible scaffolding of meals cooked, wounds tended, children raised, classrooms managed, homes kept afloat — all of it paved the way for their ascension. How do we honor the hands that feed, heal, and nurture? By demoting them, cutting their pay, and calling it “fiscal responsibility.”

Dependency Denied

Here lies the tragicomic irony: capitalism cannot survive without care work. The corporate class, the innovators, the entire workforce — they all depend on a web of invisible, feminized, often racialized labor. Yet the market, ever eager to talk about “value creation,” systematically denies its dependency.

This isn’t some unfortunate oversight; it is structural. As care work grows more precarious, it becomes the substrate upon which capitalism’s glitzy facade is built. Underpaying care workers is not enough; the system demands their delegitimization, their consignment to an underclass of “unskilled labor.” Care work is not merely forgotten — it is actively erased from the narrative of value.

Reclaiming Legitimacy: Toward a Politics of Care

Addressing this contradiction is not simply about justice for care workers — it is about our collective future. Declassifying and delegitimizing care work is a political act: it suppresses the forms of labor that resist total commodification and, in so doing, suppresses our shared humanity.

If we are to survive, let alone thrive, we must demand more than wage increases. We must revalorize care — not as a side effect of “real” work, but as the foundation of civilized life. This requires dismantling the dogma that profit alone justifies value.

The hand that feeds is tired of being bitten.

Will we finally value the work that keeps us alive, or continue to worship the altar of profit — hungry, sick, and alone?
